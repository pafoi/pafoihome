# Planting Seeds in the Classroom – A Teacher’s Story

I teach a mixed class of teenagers.

They are bright, funny, and full of potential – but over the last few years I’ve watched their energy shrink. Many of them arrive tired, anxious, or simply uninterested in yet another worksheet or lecture.

I felt it too. I found myself marking assignments late at night and thinking, “Is anyone actually alive in this process? Including me?”

---

## The question behind the lesson plans

Under the surface, I was carrying a quiet question:

> “How can my students feel more **alive** and **involved** in their own learning?”

I didn’t have a new curriculum, a new building, or more hours in the day. I just had that question and a sense that we needed to do something differently.

A colleague mentioned **Plant a Forest of Ideas** and said, “This feels like you.”

---

## Discovering PaFoI

On the PaFoI site, I saw the Seed–Plant–Forest metaphor and something clicked.

I realised that:

- My students had plenty of **seeds** – they were just rarely asked to name them.  
- We rarely gave them space to turn those seeds into **plants** – small projects that mattered to them.  
- Our classroom was mostly about **tests**, not about **forests** of ideas and relationships.

I decided to try a very small experiment.

---

## The tiny experiment: one period, one seed

Instead of my usual lesson one week, I told the class:

> “Today, I want to know what *you* are curious about. Not what the textbook says. Not what I think is important. What *you* want to explore.”

I wrote three prompts on the board:

- “I wish people talked more about…”  
- “One thing I’d love to learn or try is…”  
- “If I could change one small thing around me, it would be…”

I asked each student to choose one and write for five minutes – no marks, no right or wrong.

Then I introduced the idea of an **Idea Seed**: just a small statement of something that matters to you.

We collected their seeds anonymously at the end of class.

---

## Reading the seeds

After school, I sat at my desk and read them.

They were:

- Honest (“I wish adults didn’t only care about grades.”)  
- Practical (“I’d like to learn how to cook better so I can help at home.”)  
- Big‑hearted (“I want the new students to feel less alone.”)  
- Curious (“I wonder how my grandparents felt when they were my age.”)

I realised I had never given them this kind of space before. I felt moved and a little humbled.

---

## Turning seeds into small projects

With a little help from PaFoI guidelines, I designed a follow‑up experiment:

- Each student would pick **one seed** they cared about.  
- They would design a **tiny project** around it – something they could try over 3–4 weeks.  
- The project could be individual or with classmates.  
- The only requirement was to **reflect** on what happened.

Examples that emerged:

- A group started a “welcome buddy” idea for new students.  
- One student recorded short interviews with grandparents about their teenage years.  
- Another experimented with planning and cooking one family meal a week.  
- Two students organised a small “no marks” creativity corner after school.

We used simple **Project Notes** to keep track of what, why, and how.

---

## What changed in the classroom

It wasn’t perfect. Some projects fizzled. A few students were sceptical. We still had to prepare for exams.

But something shifted:

- The room felt more **alive**.  
- Students talked about their projects without being prompted.  
- I saw quiet students share ideas that had nothing to do with grades.  
- I felt more like a facilitator of learning, less like a machine delivering content.

At the end of the term, we held a small sharing circle. Students talked about:

- What surprised them.  
- What was harder than they thought.  
- What they might want to explore next.

Several said, “I didn’t know my own ideas mattered this much.”

---

## What I learned as a teacher

From this small experiment, I learned that:

- I don’t have to redesign the entire curriculum to make room for ideas.  
- One period and a few honest prompts can open a lot.  
- Students already have seeds; they need permission and a simple structure to plant them.  
- Learning feels different when it is connected to their real lives and feelings.

PaFoI gave me a language and a frame: **seeds, plants, forest**. The tools were simple, but they changed how I see my role.

I am not just here to deliver content. I am here to help ideas grow.

## If you’re an educator reading this

You might be:

- Tired, stretched, and wondering if you have energy for “one more thing”.  
- Worried that you don’t have time to try new approaches.  
- Unsure whether your students will take it seriously.

My experience is:

- You can start as small as **one class** and **one seed‑writing activity**.  
- You don’t need to scrap your existing syllabus.  
- The first step is simply asking, “What matters to you?”
